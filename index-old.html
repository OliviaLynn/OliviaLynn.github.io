Machines in Music 2017

Synthesizers | Exhibition | Lectures

I was lucky enough to attend 2017's Machines in Music Exhibition this past weekend, where I got to see (and get my hands on) some really cool new technology people are working on, like the --, --, and --.

David Tudor and John Cage, fiddling with homebrewed synths

The lectures were amazing, too--I think my favorite was about David Tudor, the organist/pianist who worked closesly with John Cage, and later switched to composing and creating hundreds of instruments that he would wire together to create synthesized sound through feedback loops, which is particularly fun when compared to currently popular methods like subtractive synthesis.

I have a bunch of notes of things to follow up on from that and all the other talks, which I'll come back to type up, but my notebook's at home right now.

Some other highlights of the weekend include attending the release party for DETOUR 006. I saw some familiar faces and met some really cool people, one of whom told me great things about The User Experience Team of One: A Research and Design Survival Guide. The book had made design intuitive to her, she said, even without a formal design background. I'm very excited to start reading it.

I was also fortunate enough to see Tokimonsta at the Brooklyn Electronic Music Festival, and as expected, seeing her play as if she had never had brain surgery was beyond words.
