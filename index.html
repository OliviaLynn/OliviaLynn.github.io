What I've Been Up To

AFTERIMAGE: LFG's 2018 Global Game Jam. [6/29/18]

June 29, 2018

My friend and I led development for a 2D, arcade-style game called Afterimage at a Game Jam. I hadn't built a game with a team of people in a couple of years and I came out having learned some important things:

  • Pay more attention to your improvement. I'd hardly touched Unity since 2016, but all the other programming I've done in the meantime must have translated incredibly - I spend the week noticing that things that used to bewilder me felt intuitive and comfortable this time around. It was great.
  • Leardership isn't really optional. The first time I developed a game with a team, I felt shy and timid and not fit to call any of the shots. We had six developers, and I ended up having to pull an all nighter with just one other developer to try to cobble together everyone's half-written code to get the game playable in time for judgement.
    This time around, I felt far more confident stepping up, making executive decisions, and giving out deadlines to my teammates. We ended up with a much more finished and cohesive game this time around, but we still lacked one person who was calling the shots and keeping tabs on everyone, and our performance in the last couple of days reflected that. It still feels a bit weird to step up to a leadership role in a school of such talented people, but I'm proud of the major improvement I've shown and looking forward to the next game.
  • Game Jam + Final Exam = Ow. Not the best timing. I managed to balance my time really well, but I've enjoyed sleeping so much ever since.

    SOUNDSCAPE INSTALLATION: Phipps Conservatory and Botanical Gardens. [4/28/18]

    Data Visualization: Emma Slayton at Sorrel's Library. [2/13/18]

    February 13, 2018

    This was a brief talk given by Emma Slayton, CLIR Fellow for Data Visualization at CMU.

    Unfortunately, I had to run out of a little bit early because I had a class lecture, but it was great to review some classic data visualization concepts and history. I also learned new things, too, so all in all I'd call it a success. Some highlights include:

    • Hans Rosling apparently gave a lot of TED Talks. I remember--years ago--stumbling upon some version of his classic bubble chart that shows all sorts of trends demographic data, like per-country income vs life expectancy. It's good to have a name to attach to the chart, and to know there's a larger body of work to follow up on.
    • There's a site visualizingdata.com. Of course, I guess. Glad to have it brought to my attention, there are some great resources there.
    • The Graph Continuum, a handy chart mapping the relationships between different types of graphs. It's both pretty and useful for organizing different options, and helpful for remembering the graphs you see less commonly still exist.

    Gene Kogan: Machine Learning & Art Lecture. [2/11/18]

    February 11, 2018

    It was fantastic to attend Gene Kogan's talk on Monday at CMU. He's a programmer and self-taught artist, who--for lack of better succinct wording--makes some really cool things and knows a bunch of other people who make cool things, too.

    I have a 3-page Google doc full of people, projects, and tools he mentioned that I'm really excited to check out, but some of the highlights were:

    • Kogan's made musical instruments with Jeff Snyder, the current Director of Electronic Music at Princeton University (you know, if creating a specialty controller that was commisioned by Buchla weren't impressive enough).
    • Snyder's research makes me want to curl up with some hot chocolate on a Sunday afternoon and read about cutting edge digital lutherie, and can be viewed here and here.
    • He mentioned Mario Klingemann, or Quasimondo, an artist in residence at Google Arts & Culture. Some of his pieces include generative collages in the style of Ernst, though I think I like his work with generative adversarial networks (GANs) the most--controlling a person's face and their expressions and a spooky music video that interpolates between different faces.
    • His project ml4a, a compilation of guides and demos that teach machine learning for artists (or anyone, really). Just wow.



    Music + Code: Workshop at Babycastles, New York City. [11/5/17]

    November 5, 2017

    Attended an afternoon workshop at Babycastles, a non-profit in NYC that does a lot of work teaching the public about technology and art, and supports local artists who explore this combination, too.

    The first part covered controlling p5.js sketches with arduino-style hardware. Did you know that Play-Doh is conductive? I didn't, but it sure was fun to use it to make a set of piano keys that played noise when touched. Someone brought up Dan Shiftman's video series The Nature of Code, which explores programming of natural simulations with Processing. I'd seen some of Shiftman's videos years ago, when I was first learning Processing, so I'm very happy to have this reminder and a recommendation for some more advanced concepts to explore, now that I've learned so much more.

    The second half of the workshop was a talk by Martim Galvao, a multimedia artist currently persuing PhD in Computer Music and Multimedia at Brown University. I took a lot away from the work he showed and the things he explained, but some books I'm adding to my list are Repeating Ourselves by Robert Fink ("Advertising prepared everyone for minimalist music," he said) and The Geometry of Musical Rhythm by Godfried T. Toussaint (which discusses Euclidean rhythms, among other things). Toussaint also has a published paper on Euclidean algorithms.




    MIM 2017: NYC's Modular Synthesizer Conference. [10/15/17]

    October 15, 2017

    I was lucky enough to attend 2017's Machines in Music Exhibition this past weekend, where I got to see (and get my hands on) some really cool new technology people are working on, including a bunch of experimental modules by the amazing crew at Bastl Instruments (who also let me take home their 100+ page book of modules and patch techniques they've been playing with) and Critter & Guitari's ETC video synthesizer (which also lets you program your own visual modes in Python).

    The lectures were wonderful, too--I think my favorite was about David Tudor, the organist/pianist who worked closesly with John Cage. Tutor later switched to composing and creating hundreds of instruments that he would wire together to create synthesized sound through feedback loops, which is particularly fun when compared to the usual modern methods of additive/subtractive/FM synthesis.

    Some other highlights of the weekend include attending the release party for DETOUR 006. I saw some familiar faces and met some really interesting people, one of whom told me great things about The User Experience Team of One: A Research and Design Survival Guide. The book had made design intuitive to her, she said, even without a formal design background. I put it on my To Read list right away.

    I was also fortunate enough to see Tokimonsta at the Brooklyn Electronic Music Festival, and as expected, seeing her play as if she had never had brain surgery was beyond words.


    Olivia Lynn

    I study Computer Science and Fine Art at Carnegie Mellon University.

    I love learning new ways math can be used to explain so many beautiful parts of life--and can be used to describe new things, too. These days, I can't stop thinking about sounds: how we can display them visually, how that helps us understand them better, and how that comprehension can lead to new & innovative synthesis.

    Beyond that, I am always interested in both data visualization and medicine/healthcare--how can we standardize EMRs while encouraging competition within the market? Can we find better ways to open access to research and facilitate communication among scientists, without bypassing publishers and forcing researchers to foot the cost associated with peer-reviewing and typesetting findings? Are there ways to further develop bundle payments or explore new payment plans to make care more affordable while improving hospital margins? Can we develop a site, app, periodical, or some sort of platform to compile indormation on different medical conditions and lifestyle choices empirically proven to help, to reach patients that might not have the time or ability to read articles and studies on their own? And of course, how can we continue applying our classic modern buzzwords of "big data" and "machine learning" to genuinely aide new areas of medicine?
